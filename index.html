How To Use Dichotomous Keys

Follow one yes/no choice at a time

A dichotomous key works by asking two opposite questions at every step. Students observe features such as feathers, fur, gills, moist skin, or jointed legs, then follow the matching branch until the organism is identified.

Category Ideas

Animals can be grouped in similar but different categories

Students can sort animals by backbone, skin covering, reproduction, habitat, and movement. They can also compare air-based, land-based, and water-based animals across different groups.

Vertebrate

Mammals

Have fur or hair and usually give birth to live young. Examples: dogs, humans, cats, dolphins.

Vertebrate

Reptiles

Have dry, scaly skin and usually lay eggs. Examples: snakes, lizards, turtles.

Vertebrate

Birds

Have feathers, wings, and lay eggs. Examples: eagles, sparrows, owls.

Vertebrate

Fish

Live in water and breathe with gills. Examples: salmon, goldfish, tuna.

Vertebrate

Amphibians

Often live on land and in water and have moist skin. Examples: frogs, toads, salamanders.

Invertebrate

Arthropods

Have jointed legs and an exoskeleton. Examples: insects, spiders, crabs.

Dichotomous Keys

Learn the method, then practice with an interactive yes/no key to identify an organism.

How To Use It
Step 1 Observe visible traits such as feathers, fur, skin type, legs, fins, or wings.
Step 2 Read one pair of opposite choices, then choose the option that matches the animal.
Step 3 Follow the next clue until the key reaches a final identification.
Step 4 Compare the result with real examples to check that it makes sense.
